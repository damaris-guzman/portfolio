Every Day I See My Dream

Where does the dream end and the feed begin?

Every day I see my dream unfolds as a lucid, looping fever; the screen shows me hopes I never said aloud, translated into pixels before I can claim them.

In the gallery, chrome wings cradle a fossilized SD card, promising flight yet sealing memory in a glittering reliquary. Parallel to this work and its metal cast are three additional keys, each projection-mapped with animated simulations. Scan them left to right and the sequence leaps from pure circuitry to living tissue to thinking brain, a rapid evolution that asks where technology ends and the body begins.

At the center of the room, a midi board invites visitors to remix the visuals using blooms, blurs, and glitches, yet one unmarked pad suddenly swaps in a live feed from the camera tracking the crowd, casting their own faces across the projection and revealing that the feed itself is gazing back. What seems like a personal reverie is really a feedback circuit, private thoughts returned as public spectacle.

The dream on display is vivid yet slippery, always recombining, never letting anyone hold a single shape for long, …and the room whispers a question: as our visions scroll before us, are we still the dreamers, or have we become the engine that keeps the images turning?

In short, the project is a two-part installation: a visceral film that visualizes the merger of flesh and interface, and an interactive “operational double” that lets the audience discover just how little control they truly have.

The story behind the sims ▼

The simulations visualize the divide between flesh and circuitry, letting a living body breathe in algorithmic matter until organic sensation + computer logic fuse as one. It's like visualizing a literal AI enter your body and alter it or even try to mirror you. From synthetic to organic.

So, I could 3d animate these visuals, but I choose to produce them as simulations because simulations are alive.

A 3-D animation is a pre-baked movie: once you hit "render," every pixel is locked, every frame predetermined. A simulation is the opposite, it's a living algorithm that keeps solving itself while you watch. I'm interested in that becoming. The artwork isn't a file; it's an ever-current negotiation between code, GPU, and viewer eyes.

Key-framing a 3-D scene keeps the artist in full command. But as a simulation I surrender part of that control to mathematics like diffusion and random seeds. The piece embodies N. Katherine Hayles' posthuman idea that meaning emerges when information and materiality co-construct each other. I write the rules; the computer performs them; we co-author the result.

Each visual hands a little more control to the algorithm, letting us feel how digital systems capture sensory space and, in turn, shape our nervous system, exactly like an endless social-media scroll that studies every pause and feeds it back as the next hit.

Taken together, the pieces form a kind of playground for thinking about technological embodiment. You can watch images pulse and behave like organs, feel control drift between your hand and the algorithm, and catch your own reflection inside a feedback storm because the algorithm is, in essence, mirroring you.

My goal is to evoke awareness of the fusion between internet algorithms and the technological codes that both reflect our behavior and guide it toward corporate consumption goals. I want viewers to feel closer to technology on their own terms…aware of how every touch, scroll, and click subtly engages the nervous system, shaping our psychological states and everyday decisions, for better or worse. By making data capture and behavioral nudging visible in physical space, I hope to create a moment of choice: to either continue following the script or consciously break it.

Interactive Control Panel

This ia an archived log. I have decided to use a midi board instead of designing, programming, and wiring my own audio-visual controller.

*This is a prototype. The final version may look drastically different.*

The control panel is a laser cut console filled with recycled toy buttons, arcade switches, dials, a joystick, and an Arduino LCD display. This is a prototype, so the final version will likely have fewer buttons. Most inputs are just for show; only a couple actually do anything. Each of them alter the video slightly by changing color hues, adding blur, glitching clips, etc. By allowing participant to discover which buttons work, the panel shows how technology can promise total control while giving almost none.

In this case, you're serving mine. (◕ᴗ◕✿)

Unlock-it

A 3D-printed locket frames a micro-SD card like a jewel—visible but forever sealed in resin. Part lock and part key, it keeps its data present yet unreachable, turning digital memory into a fossilized relic.

Metal Cast (Bronze)

I also 3D printed miniature versions of the locket as mold to metal cast. Pretty epic right? I got invited to share them for Stephanie Dinkin's ICA show, Data Trust, so here’s what they looked like in the show. They weren’t actually done as some pieces still needed to be cut off and polished, but it made it to the show, nonetheless.

The immersive simulations are visualized in a video I was working on to accompany all these components at once; the locket stores the immersive simulations that visualize embodiments of human–AI fusion, where flesh and digital code fuse as one. By localizing them inside a wearable object, the work compresses vast networks of computation into a personal relic.

If the simulations represent a fusion of AI, internet culture, and human embodiment, then the locket becomes a symbolic “interface-organ” where the user is fused with their data. Wearing it enacts the merger: the body is no longer distinct from the network but bound to it.

Projection Mapping Installation

Opposite Unlock-it you will find three winged keys, my Unlock-me sculptures, glowing under tightly-mapped projections from MadMapper. The moving images cling to the plastic shells, so hardware and video fuse until each pendant seems to breathe. Media thinker Katherine Hayles calls this effect "embodied virtuality," code grafted onto matter in plain sight.

Every key tells a different chapter of the same story:

Circuit / DNA hybrid
Electric-blue traces race over the wings while a twisting DNA helix glows inside the keyhole; a pulse of crimson tissue flickers beneath, hinting at what lies under every line of code.

Flesh on flesh
Two layered simulations of viscera ooze and ripple, pushing the viewer from electronics into raw biology.

Neural mesh
A shimmering mesh of lime and violet neurons fires across the surface, completing the journey from silicon to skin to intersection...or completion.

Look left to right, the sequence moves from pure circuitry to living tissue to thinking brain; a quick evolution that asks where technology ends and the body begins.

Live-thru-me: An interactive experience

A high-octane suite of simulations and animations pours molten textures across the wall, staging a collision of skin and circuitry, while a seemingly empowering MIDI console invites you to "edit" the spectacle. Each pad merely overlays visual effects, reminding us that platform "agency" lives inside a sandbox someone else has drawn. One unlabeled pad flips on a camera, loops your own image into the projection, and completes the cybernetic circuit: viewer becomes content, content measures viewer, and the feedback loop persists.

The loop asks who is dreaming whom. Algorithms sift our micro-gestures like a subconscious sorting memories, surface half-formed longings, then feed them back as bait. We appear to steer the stream, yet it already rehearses our desires and folds them into its next ripple. It feels like REM sleep with metrics: desire slips out as data, reenters from the screen, and guides perception from the outside. Are we directing the vision, or just powering the projector that keeps the simulation alive?

Ctrl-1 *Prototype* Collaboration with Meriel Hossain

Ctrl-1 is a laser cut console filled with recycled toy buttons, arcade switches, dials, a joystick, and an Arduino LCD display. This is a prototype. Most inputs are just for show; only one actually does anything. The goal was to basically create an experimental console where some buttons activate changes to a video projection while others don't. By allowing participant to discover which buttons work, the panel shows how technology can promise total control while giving almost none. Conceptually it sits beside algorithm driven platforms that mimic choice while quietly steering results to serve the platform's own goals, not the user's.

In this case, you're serving mine. (◕ᴗ◕✿)
