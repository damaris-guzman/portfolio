Where does our agency end and the algorithm take over?

Research
This project centers around a short video that explores how human cognition and digital technology intertwine, illustrating a cycle of productivity, burnout, and digital embodiment. Influenced by posthumanist thought, the project blurs the boundary between human agency and algorithmic influence, showing how technology extends and distorts self-perception.

The protagonist’s interface, layered with health metrics, emotional tracking, and a Tamagotchi-style wellness monitor, turns the body into an informatic system, always optimizing yet bound to fail. A diegetic control panel reflects the protagonist’s deteriorating mental state, with notifications interrupting at moments of peak overload, both narrative cues and commentary on the gamification of digital labor. As the protagonist snaps, the system plunges into glitch chaos, ending in a mutual burnout of mind and machine. The video is not only reflective of technological discourse but overall self-medication and addiction.

Through hyper-edited visuals, UI-driven storytelling, and reflections on digital subjectivity, “Neuro Bytes” probes the pressures of technological embodiment, revealing how humans and machines continually shape each other in an era of algorithmic acceleration.

Inside Sequence p1 (AE)

Using After Effects, I animated a shifting, interconnected “organic body” that doesn’t think in words or logic but through movement and transformation. It represents an AI with a consciousness, inside an organic body. AI as something that is constantly adapting and functions like an unfolding process. The animation is fluid, responsive, almost organic in its behavior, rejecting the idea of AI as rigid, mechanical intelligence. It’s important to note that no, i didn’t use AI for any of these visuals. Made entirely in ae.

Interactive Control Panel

This piece will be fabricated using laser cutters, 3D printers, Arduino LCD displays, and old buttons from kid toys.

The control panel symbolizes technology’s illusion of control. Although the main character appears to adjust her internal state, monitoring and manipulating focus or mood, the panel itself ultimately does nothing.

This parallels our perceived agency over internet use, despite hidden systems like data collection and addictive design that quietly shape our behavior.

The panel becomes an “operational double,” giving the appearance of control while never actually altering reality.
