Neuro Bytes
Where does our agency end and the algorithm take over?

This project pairs a looping short video to a chunky arcade control panel to show how digital systems promise choice while steering behavior. On screen a user reaches for the panel, the camera dives through their eyes into tissue and circuit paths, then lands inside a computer interface where folders flicker between Project_Focus, User_Data, and Self.exe; an error warning shatters into pixels, ads push a “Premium Focus+” upgrade, and the loop restarts (kind of like doom-scrolling).

In the gallery only a few buttons on the laser-cut console trigger brief audio-visual changes before control snaps back, mirroring platforms that harvest data yet decide what we see.

In short, the project is a two-part installation: a visceral film that visualizes the merger of flesh and interface, and an interactive “operational double” that lets the audience discover just how little control they truly have.

Technological embodiment, immersion, posthumanism, cybernetic loops

Inside Sequence p1 (AE)

Using After Effects, I animated a shifting, interconnected “organic body” that doesn’t think in words or logic but through movement and transformation. It represents an AI with a consciousness, inside an organic body. AI as something that is constantly adapting and functions like an unfolding process. The animation is fluid, responsive, almost organic in its behavior, rejecting the idea of AI as rigid, mechanical intelligence. It’s important to note that no, i didn’t use AI for any of these visuals. Made entirely in ae.

Inside Sequence p2 (AE)

But If AI’s perception of the world is shaped by data sets extracted from human activity - then its understanding of reality is a distorted, reconstructed version of us. AI doesn’t “see” the way we do; it predicts, interpolates, and generates. If human perception is mediated through our senses, AI’s perception is mediated through algorithms.

Interactive Control Panel

*This is a prototype. The final version may look drastically different.*

The control panel is a laser cut console filled with recycled toy buttons, arcade switches, dials, a joystick, and an Arduino LCD display. This is a prototype, so the final version will likely have fewer buttons. Most inputs are just for show; only a couple actually do anything. Each of them alter the video slightly by changing color hues, adding blur, glitching clips, etc. By allowing participant to discover which buttons work, the panel shows how technology can promise total control while giving almost none. Conceptually it sits beside algorithm driven platforms that mimic choice while quietly steering results to serve the platform’s own goals, not the user’s.
